What are Black Holes?

A black hole is a region in space with gravity so intense that nothing, not even light, can escape from it once within the event horizon. It is formed by the collapse of massive stars or other cosmic events that compress matter to a singularity.

  • Event Horizon: The boundary beyond which escape is impossible.
  • Singularity: A point of infinite density at the black hole's core.
  • Not solid: The event horizon is not a surface but a theoretical boundary.

How Do Scientists Detect Black Holes?

Because black holes do not emit light themselves, scientists detect them using indirect evidence:

  • Accretion Disks: Hot glowing gas swirling around emits X-rays.
  • Star Orbits: Stars orbiting an invisible massive object.
  • Gravitational Waves: Detected ripples in spacetime from black hole mergers.
  • Gravitational Lensing: Light bending around black holes distorts background objects.

How Are Black Holes Formed?

Black holes usually form when massive stars collapse after supernova explosions, creating a singularity with an event horizon.

  • Stellar-mass black holes: Result from dying stars with masses over ~8-10 times the Sun.
  • Supermassive black holes: Found in galaxy centers; formed by gas accretion and mergers.
  • Primordial black holes: Theoretical black holes formed shortly after the Big Bang.

What Happens Inside a Black Hole?

Inside a black hole, matter is crushed to an infinitely dense point called a singularity, where known laws of physics break down. The event horizon marks the boundary beyond which nothing can escape, and spacetime is extremely warped. Classical general relativity predicts the singularity, but quantum gravity effects likely play a role at this scale, though details remain unknown.

  • Singularity: Point of infinite density and zero volume.
  • Spacetime curvature: Near-infinite; classical physics ceases to apply.
  • Event horizon: No information escapes beyond this boundary.
  • The true internal structure is still a subject of research.
